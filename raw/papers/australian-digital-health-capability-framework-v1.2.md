Introduction
Digital health is a critical part of the modern healthcare industry. Its adoption is key to
optimising access to services, the way health and care operates, and enabling new ways
of delivering services and care.
For the health industry to fully embrace the digital evolution of the sector, it is vital that the
health and care workforce has the capability to effectively understand, use, and apply
digital technolologies in the context of their work.
The need for a “digtally ready and enabled health and wellbeing workforce” is recognised
in Australia’s National Digital Health Strategy 2023-2028. 1
The Australasian Institute of Digital Health (AIDH) has been engaged by the Australian
Digital Health Agency (‘the Agency’) to develop the Australian Digital Health Capability
Framework. This framework aims to facilitate the advancement, growth, and evaluation of
workforce knowledge and skills in the field of digital health.

What is Digital Health?
Digital health is a broad term incorporating the movement of health and care into an  ever-
evolving digital society.
It can be referred to as “a range of technologies that can be used to treat patients and
collect and share a person’s health information...and includes mobile health apps, electronic
prescribing, electronic health records, telehealth, wearables and fitness trackers, robotics
and artificial intelligence, among others. 6”
For the purposes of this Framework, digital health is described as health and care in the
context of digital societies (i.e. the people, organisations and things engaged in digital
interactions in ever-increasing ways).7 This trend in digital is happening in society and will
continue to happen in health and care, where our lives will be enabled by or have some
digital context.
Digital change is the driver and enabler of the shift in health and care towards greater
consumer-centred services and personalised care. This is what consumers and society are
demanding. It includes technologies, devices, and systems, as well as digital data and
analytics, all of which are advancing and evolving rapidly. It promises advanced treatments
and improved health and care experiences for health practitioners, patients, and consumers
as we move towards more accessible and personalised delivery of health services.
“Digital health is the foundation for all modern health service delivery
and should improve safety, quality, productivity and efficiency." 1
The need for an Australian Digital Health
Capability Framework
Digital health is not just about the use of technology, but about improving the delivery of
health and care services.
The framework provides overarching guidance and consistent way of thinking about
workforce capability in digital health. Whilst the framework has been developed specifically
for the health sector, it could equally apply to the health and care sector.
The National Digital Health Strategy 2023-2028 is a five-year plan that sets the vision and
pathway for Australia’s Digital health future. This is a national strategy that has been developed
in consultation with all Australian governments, consumers, healthcare providers and the
software industry. The vision is an ‘inclusive, sustainable and healthier future for all Australians
through a connected and digitally enabled health system’. A key priority is a health workforce
competent and confident in delivering health and care using digital health.1


Australasian Institute of Digital Health    4
Achieving this requires the definition of a national set of digital health capabilities towards
which health workers of all types can professionally aspire, and on which workplaces can
focus to build capability consistently across staff. A standardised, profession-agnostic
approach will ensure consistency across the sector and increase opportunity for the
development of transferable digital health capabilities which can be applied across a range
of roles and settings.
Health consumers increasingly want to access their data through digital channels. In the
2021 National Digital Health Strategy ‘Have your Say’ Survey, 88% of the 6,622 respondents
indicated a desire for digital access to their personal health information.1 A workforce
confident and capable in the use of digital health is vital to ensure that the health consumer
can remain at the centre of the framework and ensure their desires and expectations can
be met now and into the future.
“Digital technologies and data provide people with better access to their
health information, better quality care when and where they need it, and
more seamless and personalised health outcomes.” 1
The Australian Digital Health Capability Framework has been developed in consultation with
key stakeholders and informed by existing resources including the National Nursing and
Midwifery Digital Health Capability Framework (2020), authored by the Australian Digital
Health Agency and published in 2020.
Changes were made to existing frameworks to ensure applicability across all health
workers and work settings. In addition, integrated feedback from key stakeholders was
incorporated to inform a consumer-centred framework relevant to current and future
approaches across the sector.
Purpose of the Framework
The Australian Digital Health Workforce Capability Framework is intended to act as a
practical guide for organisations and individuals on the skills and knowledge required to
effectively deliver health care in an increasingly digital world. It aims to:
•
encourage the informed and critical use of digital technologies in the delivery of health
and care services,
•
contribute to improving the collection, management and use of administrative and
clinical data,
•
influence enhanced service and care interactions between health and care providers
and consumers
We need consistency in our language and approach to influence efforts
towards health system sustainability.

The audience for the Framework. Who is part
of the ‘health workforce’?
The Australian Digital Health Agency describes the ‘health workforce’ as
‘all individuals who deliver or assist in the delivery of health services or
support the operation of health care facilities’. 1
This definition includes health care professionals, such as primary and community care
health workers, public health officers, and all other workers in the health system such
as administrators, managers, and health technology professionals.
The health workforce also includes digital health specialists, such as health information
officers, health data scientists, health information managers, health informaticians,
health information technologists, and so on. These specialists work as practitioners,
policy-makers, innovators, educators and researchers.
Therefore, the audience for this Framework is broad and includes:
•
Administrative workers and managers in health and care organisations
•
Health professionals across medical, nursing, allied health, community, and aged
care
•
Other health workers in monitoring, support and care roles, including hospital/health
librarians
•
Technical workers across digital health, including ICT etc.
•
Researchers working with health information, knowledge, and technology beyond the
scope of health data
•
Educators working with health data, information, knowledge and technology, or
educators training or teaching healthcare professionals or care workers

How the Framework Will Be Used
The Australian Digital Health Capability Framework is designed as a means
for progressive upskilling in individuals and as a platform for the creation
of new models of health delivery in organisations committed to safe and
seamless consumer-centred care.
The Framework provides a foundation for digital health workforce capability programs
nationally, both in assessing existing capabilities and planning for the training and
assessment of new ones. Importantly, the Framework provides a common language and
set of concepts and descriptions to inform discussions about digital health capabilities
across the health workforce.
This Framework deals specifically with digital health capabilities – it is not intended to
address capabilities relating to basic computer literacy or profession-specific skillsets.
Further, the Framework is underpinned by the following assumptions:
•
Organisations and individuals, including workers and patients/consumers, have
varying levels of digital literacy and access to technology.
•
The health workforce and consumers increasingly work and live in a digitally enabled
environment which must be used to optimise health processes and outcomes.
•
Every health and care worker has the inherent capability to develop the knowledge
and skills to use information and communication technologies and digital data, to
optimise health processes and outcomes.
Scope and Assumptions
The Framework is intended to be applicable across the health workforce and all health
and care settings. It is intended to augment existing profession-specific resources,
however the context in which different professions use the Framework may vary.

Acknowledging professional standards,
workplace settings, and our different roles
The Framework acknowledges the complexity of the workforce and the variety of
working environments and roles across health and care settings. This includes:
•
Professional standards – relevant to our unique roles in health and care, whether they
be clinical, technical, administrative, or research. There may be professional standards
and education requirements for specific professions, which this Framework supports
and aligns with, but does not replace.
•
Workplace settings  – health organisations will have specific regulations, policies, rules,
and priorities for their staff which the Framework will support and provide additional
information in the context of digital health.
•
Roles – we all have different roles, responsibilities, and levels of influence. The
Framework acknowledges this and provides a way for individuals to work with their own
professional development on how they can be the best in their role using digital health.
The Framework is not meant to replace existing Frameworks or requirements in professional
standards. Rather it supports, informs, and influences the success of those with the
addition of digital health capabilities and a consistent way of thinking about digital health
when it comes to the workforce.
The Framework will also provide the basis for the future design of a suite of tools and
resources targeted at health and care organisations and health and care professionals to
help realise the goal of building digital health workforce capability. We anticipate the
following intended uses:
•
For organisations and individuals to assess their own digital health capabilities and
identify workforce learning and development needs.
•
To inform personal and professional development plans relevant to current or future
workplace roles.
•
For health services to use as part of their continuous quality improvement activities
to assess organisational capacity and educational requirements.
•
For educators and trainers to inform curriculum and courses.
•
To develop tools to assist in extending workforce digital health capabilities.
•
To inform pathways for career advancement in digital health.
•
Inform organisations and health educators on the capability required of workers in
health and care to deliver safe and seamless consumer-centred care. To increase
awareness of and facilitate interoperability between digital technologies.

The Framework
The Framework consists of five domains which support the National Digital Health Strategy of
‘an inclusive, sustainable and healthier future for all Australians through a connected and
digitally enabled health system’. 1
The five domains provide a standard and consistent approach to capabilities in digital health
which can be applied across a range of people working in health and care, including clinical,
non-clinical, technical, research, administrative, educational and support areas.
The health workforce group who are digital health specialists will have advanced capabilities
that align with internationally authoritative recommendations and standards for digital health
practice. These are out of scope in the present framework. The Framework acknowledges the
varied and different professional standards, workplace settings, and roles across health and
care. It acknowledges that individuals and groups will view a standard and generic framework
looking for context in daily work and professional development.
The Framework should inform and influence how individuals can lift and build their
understanding and capability in digital health to be confident in their role using digital
technologies and solutions. As well as providing organisations, educators, and other
stakeholders the ability to understand and build digital health capability in their workforce and
professional groups in the key areas listed in the domains.

The Framework structure provides five Domains (A-E) with three sub-domains under

Capability Levels
Each capability statement has three levels which depict the anticipated growth of
knowledge, skills, and abilities in the capability area. The degree of capability at each level
is illustrated by the level indicator statements. These statements are not intended to be an
exhaustive list, and every indicator is not necessarily relevant to every role or work setting.
The three levels do not correspond to employment level or position, as different roles and
settings may require and support capabilities at different levels. Most workers should aspire
to achieving the highest level of capability possible relating to their role and work setting.
Capabilities associated with formal certifications or recognised qualifications in digital
health and informatics fall outside the scope of this Framework. Those specialising in digital
health and seeking to obtain formal qualifications or certification should refer to the relevant
professional body or association for guidance.

The Framework isn’t just about digital health capabilities, it is a foundation for the delivery
of health and care, now and into the future. Developing a digitally capable health workforce
is an ongoing process and organisations will require a way to track impact and measure
improvements over time.
Organisations can use the Australian Digital Health Capability Framework (ADHCF) to
support their journey to a more digitally capable workforce. Supporting resources are
available to guide the application of the Framework and include:
This Framework focuses on capability in the workforce as it relates to digital health.
Capability addresses professionalism in terms of an individual’s life-long learning,
adaptability to change, and self-efficacy. This is relevant for individual workers as our
health sector and supporting systems change increasingly to using digital solutions in the
workplace.
Therefore, capability frameworks like the Australian Digital Health Capability Framework
focus on supporting continuous development in individuals and the workforce, rather than
a formal assessment of a skill at a specific point in time. It is focused on improvement,
development, and building understanding and capability to be the best in your role using
digital health.
This contrasts with competency which is the specification of knowledge and skill to a role,
and the application of that knowledge and skill, to a standard of performance expected
in the workplace. In other words, knowledge and skills, when applied in context, support
the demonstration of competence. This Framework supports workplace standards and
competency requirements but does not seek to measure performance against individual
roles and responsibilities.

Glossary
Care services
This is a broader term that encompasses not just medical treatment but
also support and assistance for people with daily living needs. It might refer
to long-term care, such as what’s offered in nursing homes, or support for
those with disabilities or chronic conditions. Examples include home care
aides helping with personal hygiene, meal preparation for the elderly, or
palliative care for terminally ill patients.
Data
Facts that can be interpreted or analysed to gain knowledge or aid
decision-making.
Data Integrity/Quality
Data integrity is the accuracy, completeness, and quality of data as it’s
maintained over time and across formats. Data quality measures how well
a dataset meets criteria for accuracy, completeness, validity, consistency,
uniqueness, timeliness, and fitness for purpose, and it is critical to all data
governance initiatives within an organisation.
Digital technologies
Broad term for the variety of technologies that use computers and
computerisation.
Emerging Technology
Fast growing innovations which are in the early stages of acceptance or
adoption.
Health Services
Refers specifically to services that are related to the diagnosis, treatment,
and prevention of diseases, illnesses, injuries, and other physical and mental
impairments. Examples include doctor visits, surgical procedures, laboratory
testing, and vaccinations.
Information
Processed or analysed data that now have relevance for a specific purpose
or context.
Interoperability
The ability to exchange and use information across different networks and
applications.
